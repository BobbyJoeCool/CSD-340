Prologue

The entire village of ________ was gathered at the edge of the town square, where the atmosphere was set for a party, lit lanterns, tables with food and drink, and music being played by the bards. All this to bid farewell to one of their own. _______ was preparing for his ____.

Most come back after a year or two with stories to tell of the wider world. A few never return. If three years pass with no return, with or without word, they are declared dead, mourned at the next festival, and quietly removed from the town's registry. Tonight was _______'s turn. However, while the rest of ________ got drunk and sang farewell songs, two figures stood beneath a grove of snow-crowned pines, arguing in hushed tones that didn't match the mood.

“I still don't understand why you have to do this ridiculous tradition,” ____ Felis hissed, tail lashing. Her black fur, which was always a little ruffled when she was anxious, was practically standing on end.

“Because I want to see the world with my own eyes,” _______ said, his tone gentle but unwavering. His silver scales almost blended in with the snow on the ground.

____'s ears twitched with frustration. “You can see the world in the libraries. You can learn everything from the scrolls and tomes and maps, and you can stay safe while doing it. Why risk everything for something you could read about?” Silly stupid risks. Why risk your life when everything you need is right here, she thought.

“Because reading about the ocean isn't the same as smelling salt on the wind.” _______ smiled. “Because the world is real out there, ____. And I want to understand it, not just study it.”

How can he be so calm about this? she thought. “Then take me with you,” ____ snapped, though she regretted it the instant it left her mouth.

_______'s smile curved upward. “Please do. Keep me safe. Keep me out of trouble.”

She glared at him. “You know I can't. You know why.”

The memory of many council meetings where ____ raised her objects to the ____ came to her memory. Not worth the risk, she thought, thinking of friends who never returned home. She was dismissed every time in the name of tradition.

_______ folded his arms. “Then maybe you should stay here and keep the Council from kicking me out of the town records.”

That landed. They stood in silence for a long moment, the murmur of distant music rising from the village square behind them. Fireworks started to go off in the distance. If ever there's an excuse for wizards to show off with a fireworks display, they're going to take it, thought ____.

“I don't want to fight,” _______ said at last, more softly. “I'm leaving town. Not just because it's what we do here. I want to see the places you read about. The places you made me dream about. I want to learn from more than just the elders. I want to know what the world is really like, and bring that knowledge home. I want to be able to write one of those scrolls that tells our future generations how the world runs.” He stepped closer, his voice now barely above a whisper. “I will come back, ____. I promise.”

____ swallowed. Her claws flexed around the hem of her cloak. Her heart was a tangle of thorns, fear, pride, guilt, and perhaps even love. This might be the last time I ever see him, she thought, and I'm wasting it on an argument.

“You're right, of course,” she said, forcing a smile. “Go. Make your memories. And bring back the stories.”

_______'s face softened. “You'll miss me.”

“Not a chance,” she said. But her voice cracked, just slightly.
