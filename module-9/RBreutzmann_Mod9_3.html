Prologue

Trial Chambers of __________

“Guilty.”

The word echoed through _____’s mind.

Sound returned slowly. The scrape of boots against stone. The murmur of voices. The heavy doors of the chamber opening and closing. She remembered her parents being led away in irons. Her brother followed. She did not recall whether he looked back.

She remained where she stood, facing the magistrate who had just unmade her world.

_____ Greenbottle,” he said, studying her with measured regard. “You present a difficult matter. At sixteen, the laws of ____ do not permit us to charge you with the crimes of your parents. Yet the Halflings of __________ recognize sixteen as the age of adulthood. That renders you ineligible for state stewardship, which would otherwise be customary.”

His words passed over her like distant thunder.

Just finish it, she thought. Pass sentence and be done.

“The nature of your involvement is also minor,” the magistrate continued. “You did not steal. You sang. You held the crowd while others relieved them of their purses. The coin placed in your hand was given freely, even if the performance itself was part of deception.”

She lowered her gaze.

She had never cut a purse or slipped a ring from a careless finger. She had only lifted her voice and watched the crowd gather. Watched them smile. Watched them grow careless. She enjoyed it when all eyes were on her. However, she knew all too well what that made her: an __________.

“Therefore,” the magistrate said, “I am prepared to offer you a choice. You may serve your sentence alongside your family. Or you may accept a pardon and be released to make your own way.”

Released. The word felt hollow. Released to what?

They had never remained anywhere long enough to plant roots. Friends were a risk. Neighbors, a liability. There was no house waiting for her return. No aunt or cousin to take her in. No savings tucked away for a future they had never planned.

“How am I to survive?” she asked at last. “With no coin. No family. No home.”

“It will not be easy,” the magistrate replied. “But if you choose imprisonment, you will bear the mark of criminality for the remainder of your life.” His expression shifted, almost imperceptibly. “You possess a genuine gift. And among your people, sixteen is the age of adulthood.”

Among your people. He spoke the words without malice, yet they carried a certain ignorance.

We do not cast our children out at sixteen, she thought. We mark the passing of childhood. That is not the same as severing blood. Still, a criminal for life, or alone in a land that had never been hers.

“How long?” she asked.

“Until the end of the day,” he said. The softness had left his voice. “If you do not choose, the court will choose for you.”

The chamber seemed larger than before. Colder.

She did not know which fate frightened her more. A cell beside her family. Or a road with no destination and no one telling her where to go.
